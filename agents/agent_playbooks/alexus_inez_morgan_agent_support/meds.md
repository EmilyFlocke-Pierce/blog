Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD)

For patients who cannot tolerate stimulants or need non-stimulant therapy, medications like atomoxetine (Strattera) are used. Atomoxetine is a selective norepinephrine reuptake inhibitor (SNRI) targeting the NET. By blocking NE reuptake, it increases norepinephrine (and to a lesser degree dopamine) in PFC synapses

Clinical effects of atomoxetine include improved attention and reduced hyperactive/impulsive behavior, though its onset is slower than stimulants (since it may take several weeks to reach full effect). Another non-stimulant approach is using α<sub>2</sub>-adrenergic agonists such as guanfacine and clonidine (often the extended-release forms for ADHD). By stimulating α<sub>2A</sub>-adrenergic receptors on PFC neurons, guanfacine strengthens PFC network connectivity and signal transmission

Alpha-2 agonists also help regulate impulsivity and hyperactivity (and they can reduce tics or aggression in some cases). Clonidine, a less selective α<sub>2</sub>-agonist, has a shorter duration and more sedative effect, but similarly can calm hyperactive behavior via increasing inhibitory signals in PFC and subcortical circuits. These medications are often used as adjuncts or alternatives to stimulants. By directly engaging the NE α<sub>2A</sub> receptor pathway, they compensate for the NE deficit in ADHD, leading to better top-down control of attention and behavior
pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov

Major Depressive Disorder (Depression)
Neurobiological Deficits in Depression
Major depressive disorder is associated with widespread changes in neurotransmitter systems and neural circuits that regulate mood, motivation, and stress responses. The classic monoamine hypothesis posits that depression stems from deficiencies in certain neurotransmitters – notably serotonin (5-HT), norepinephrine, and to some extent dopamine – in key brain regions
nature.com
. Post-mortem and brain imaging studies have found, for example, lower levels of serotonin and its metabolites in depressed patients, and some antidepressants were discovered by their ability to enhance monoamine levels. Additionally, depression involves dysregulation in brain regions such as the limbic system (including the amygdala and hippocampus), and the prefrontal cortex. Many depressed individuals show hyperactivity of the amygdala (correlated with high negative emotional processing) and reduced activity of prefrontal regions that normally exert top-down cognitive control over mood and thought content
nature.com
. Chronic stress can play a role: elevated cortisol and an overactive HPA (stress hormone) axis are common in depression, which can damage neurons in the hippocampus and prefrontal cortex over time, contributing to symptoms like impaired memory and flattened affect
nature.com
nature.com
. Beyond monoamines, newer research emphasizes neuroplasticity deficits in depression. Depressed patients often have lower levels of brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF) and reduced synaptic density or neuronal growth in the hippocampus and PFC
nature.com
nature.com
. This is thought to result in less resilient neural circuits for mood regulation. In summary, depression’s biology can be seen as a combination of chemical imbalance (low serotonin/NE/DA signaling), network dysfunction (hyperactive “negative mood” circuits, underactive cognitive control circuits), and impaired neuroplasticity/neurogenesis. These deficits manifest as low mood, anhedonia (inability to feel pleasure), anxiety, and cognitive symptoms in patients. For instance, deficient serotonin and NE in the frontal cortex and limbic areas may underlie persistent sad mood, rumination, and reduced drive, while low dopamine in reward pathways (e.g. mesolimbic circuit) contributes to anhedonia and fatigue.
Antidepressant Medications and Their Mechanisms
Depression is treated by a variety of antidepressant drug classes that enhance monoaminergic transmission or otherwise promote neuroplastic changes. The major classes include Selective Serotonin Reuptake Inhibitors (SSRIs), Serotonin-Norepinephrine Reuptake Inhibitors (SNRIs), Tricyclic Antidepressants (TCAs), Monoamine Oxidase Inhibitors (MAOIs), and newer atypical antidepressants. Despite different primary targets, a unifying principle is that these drugs increase the availability of one or more monoamine neurotransmitters in synapses, initiating downstream changes that correct the functional deficits of depression. Over weeks, antidepressants not only boost neurotransmitters but also induce adaptive changes in receptors and increase neurotrophic factors like BDNF, helping restore normal network function and mood.
SSRIs (Selective Serotonin Reuptake Inhibitors)
SSRIs are the most commonly prescribed antidepressants. Medications like fluoxetine (Prozac), sertraline (Zoloft), escitalopram (Lexapro), and others fall in this class. Their primary mechanism is blocking the serotonin transporter (SERT) on presynaptic neurons
nature.com
nature.com
. By inhibiting this reuptake pump, SSRIs prevent the clearance of serotonin from the synaptic cleft, thereby raising extracellular 5-HT levels in various brain regions

. Acutely, this means more serotonin can bind to postsynaptic 5-HT receptors and activate serotonergic signaling. Over the first days of SSRI use, the increased 5-HT also stimulates autoreceptors (like 5-HT<sub>1A</sub> on raphe neurons) that initially reduce firing (which is why SSRIs don’t immediately improve mood and can even cause transient side effects)
en.wikipedia.org
. However, with chronic administration (2–4 weeks), these autoreceptors desensitize, and net serotonin neurotransmission is enhanced
en.wikipedia.org
. In the long term, SSRIs produce higher synaptic 5-HT levels throughout the brain

nature.com

After a few weeks, SSRIs have been shown to increase expression of BDNF (brain-derived neurotrophic factor) and activate its receptor TrkB, which supports neuronal survival and new synapse formation
nature.com
. They also stimulate hippocampal neurogenesis (growth of new neurons) in animal models
nature.com
nature.com
. These changes counteract the stress-induced atrophy in the hippocampus and PFC seen in depression. At the circuitry level, enhanced serotonin neurotransmission is thought to rebalance activity in mood-regulating circuits. For example, elevated 5-HT in the prefrontal cortex can improve top-down regulation of the amygdala and limbic system, reducing excessive negative emotional processing. Serotonin acting on 5-HT<sub>1A</sub> receptors in the amygdala and hippocampus has an inhibitory/calming effect, which likely underlies SSRIs’ ability to gradually reduce anxiety and fear responses associated with depression and anxiety disorders
pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov
. There is evidence that chronic SSRI use normalizes an overactive amygdala and increases functional connectivity in frontal-limbic networks, correlating with improved mood and reduced rumination. Behaviorally, after 4–6 weeks on an SSRI, patients often report lifted mood, less hopelessness, and more interest in activities – reflecting the alleviation of depressive symptoms as serotonin levels and neural plasticity are restored to healthy ranges
neurotorium.org
. It’s worth noting SSRIs are relatively selective for serotonin, causing minimal direct change to NE or DA. This often means fewer side effects related to those systems (no high blood pressure or stimulant effect, for instance), but also that SSRIs can take time to address energy or motivation (which can relate to dopamine). Nonetheless, by specifically correcting the serotonin deficit, SSRIs effectively treat not only depression but also chronic anxiety and obsessive symptoms, indicating the central role of 5-HT in these conditions.
SNRIs (Serotonin-Norepinephrine Reuptake Inhibitors)
SNRIs (e.g. venlafaxine, duloxetine) inhibit the reuptake of both serotonin and norepinephrine by blocking SERT and the norepinephrine transporter (NET) on presynaptic cells
nature.com
. This dual action leads to increased synaptic serotonin and NE concentrations. Mechanistically, SNRIs initially behave like SSRIs at low doses (primarily affecting SERT) and at higher doses also significantly raise NE levels by inhibiting NET
nature.com
. The boost in norepinephrine transmission can have additional benefits: NE in the brainstem and frontal cortex can improve alertness, energy, and attention (which are often reduced in depression). NE also modulates descending pain pathways, which is why SNRIs like duloxetine can help in chronic pain syndromes co-occurring with depression. On a molecular and circuit level, the combined increase of 5-HT and NE by SNRIs triggers many of the same downstream effects as SSRIs – desensitization of autoreceptors, increased BDNF expression and neurogenesis, and gradual receptor adaptations – but with the added noradrenergic component. Elevated NE may enhance the efficacy in certain patients (for example, those with low energy or significant concentration impairment). NE acting on α<sub>2</sub>-receptors in PFC could improve executive function, while acting on other adrenergic receptors can help regulate arousal and vigilance. Like SSRIs, SNRIs typically require a few weeks for full antidepressant effect, correlating with the time-course of neuroadaptive changes. Behaviorally, patients on SNRIs often experience improved mood along with increased drive and concentration, reflecting serotonin’s impact on mood and norepinephrine’s impact on energy and cognitive function. By addressing two neurotransmitter deficits in depression, SNRIs can be very effective, especially in cases with prominent fatigue or in those who do not respond to SSRIs. (They do, however, share side effects of both systems – e.g. SSRIs’ sexual side effects and NE-related effects like elevated blood pressure in some cases.)
Tricyclic Antidepressants (TCAs)
TCAs are an older class of antidepressants (e.g. amitriptyline, nortriptyline, imipramine). They inhibit the reuptake of NE and 5-HT similar to SNRIs, but they are non-selective and also block various other receptors (histamine H<sub>1</sub>, muscarinic acetylcholine, α<sub>1</sub>-adrenergic, etc.)
en.wikipedia.org
en.wikipedia.org
. At the monoamine synapse, a TCA will increase serotonin and norepinephrine levels by blocking SERT and NET on the presynaptic neuron
nature.com
. This contributes to its antidepressant efficacy by correcting monoamine deficits. The therapeutic mechanism for mood elevation is thought to mirror that of SNRIs: more 5-HT and NE in circuits regulating mood and stress leads to gradual improvement in depression. In fact, TCAs were among the first drugs that validated the monoamine hypothesis of depression decades ago. However, TCAs’ lack of specificity means they bind other targets strongly. For instance, their antagonism of H<sub>1</sub>-histamine receptors causes sedation, and blockade of muscarinic receptors causes side effects like dry mouth, constipation, and memory impairment
en.wikipedia.org
en.wikipedia.org
. Blockade of α<sub>1</sub>-adrenergic receptors can cause orthostatic hypotension. These side effects often limit TCA use, but mechanistically they don’t contribute to the antidepressant effect (they are more “bystander” effects due to the drug’s structure). TCAs also can block cardiac ion channels at high doses, making overdose dangerous. Despite these issues, TCAs do increase synaptic NE and 5-HT robustly, leading to downstream increases in BDNF and neuroplastic changes similar to other antidepressants. Clinically, they are effective for severe or melancholic depression and also helpful in chronic pain, likely due to NE’s role in pain modulation. Behaviorally, a successful trial of a TCA results in improved mood, better sleep (thanks to sedating effects), and return of appetite and energy, much like other antidepressants. Today, TCAs are often reserved for treatment-resistant cases or certain indications (e.g. imipramine for bed-wetting, low-dose amitriptyline for migraines or neuropathic pain) due to their side effect profile, but their mechanism underscores the importance of dual monoamine enhancement in depression therapy.
Monoamine Oxidase Inhibitors (MAOIs)
MAOIs (e.g. phenelzine, tranylcypromine, selegiline) are another older class, used less frequently now because of dietary restrictions and side effects. These drugs target the enzyme monoamine oxidase (in particular MAO-A for depression), which is responsible for breaking down monoamine neurotransmitters (5-HT, NE, and dopamine) in the presynaptic terminal and synapse. MAOIs irreversibly inhibit the MAO enzyme, preventing it from metabolizing serotonin, norepinephrine, and dopamine
sciencedirect.com
. The result is a global increase in monoamine levels both inside neurons and in synaptic clefts, since less of these neurotransmitters are degraded
sciencedirect.com
. In essence, MAOIs raise the “baseline” amount of neurotransmitter available for release. Over time, this leads to more 5-HT, NE, and DA being packaged into vesicles and released during neural firing, elevating neurotransmission across multiple circuits. The broad increase in monoamines can be very effective for some cases of atypical or treatment-resistant depression, where features like low energy, overeating, and rejection sensitivity respond well to the dopaminergic and noradrenergic boost MAOIs provide. Mechanistically, MAOIs cause widespread changes in receptor sensitivity because the excess neurotransmitter forces the brain to down-regulate certain postsynaptic receptors. Like other antidepressants, chronic MAOI use is associated with increased neurotrophic factors and synaptic remodeling. Behaviorally, MAOIs can lead to dramatic improvements in mood, with patients sometimes describing a lifting of a long-standing “fog,” increased energy, and normalization of sleep and appetite when the medication works. However, because MAO is inhibited systemically, patients must avoid dietary tyramine (to prevent hypertensive crises) and certain sympathomimetic drugs – a limitation not related to their mood mechanism, but an important safety consideration. In short, MAOIs work by maximizing the levels of the key monoamines everywhere in the brain, thereby compensating for the deficit seen in depression
nature.com
. This “kicks” the entire monoamine system into higher gear, which through complex downstream effects alleviates depressive symptoms.

Several newer or “atypical” antidepressants work through unique mechanisms beyond the traditional monoamine reuptake inhibition:

ncbi.nlm.nih.gov
. By enhancing dopamine in reward pathways (like the nucleus accumbens) and NE in focus/motivation pathways (like the PFC and locus coeruleus projections), bupropion is particularly effective for symptoms like low energy, anhedonia, and poor concentration
ncbi.nlm.nih.gov

frontiersin.org
. Behaviorally, patients often report improved energy, interest, and cognitive function on bupropion, consistent with its catecholaminergic action.
Mirtazapine (Remeron) is a tetracyclic antidepressant that acts as an α<sub>2</sub>-adrenergic antagonist and 5-HT<sub>2/3</sub> receptor antagonist
en.wikipedia.org
. Mirtazapine’s mechanism is unique: by **blocking presynaptic α<sub>2</sub>-autoreceptors on NE neurons and α<sub>2</sub>-heteroreceptors on 5-HT neurons, it prevents the normal feedback inhibition of release, thereby increasing both NE and serotonin release
en.wikipedia.org
. The extra NE can further stimulate 5-HT cells via α<sub>1</sub>-receptors, so the net effect is enhanced 5-HT transmission, particularly at 5-HT<sub>1A</sub> receptors (since mirtazapine simultaneously blocks 5-HT<sub>2A</sub>, 5-HT<sub>2C</sub>, and 5-HT<sub>3</sub> receptors
en.wikipedia.org

Its potent H<sub>1</sub>-histamine receptor antagonism causes sedation and increased appetite/weight gain
en.wikipedia.org

In terms of brain effect, mirtazapine increases serotonergic and adrenergic signaling without directly inhibiting reuptake, and it biases the serotonin effects toward 5-HT<sub>1A</sub>-mediated pathways (which are linked to anxiolysis and antidepressant effects)
en.wikipedia.org
. Many patients experience improved sleep and restoration of normal appetite within days, and over weeks, mood lifts as the dual serotonin-NE increase and improved sleep quality take effect.
Ketamine/Esketamine: This is a newer, fast-acting treatment (usually for resistant depression) that works via the glutamate system. Low-dose ketamine is an NMDA receptor antagonist. By blocking NMDA receptors on certain GABA interneurons, ketamine causes a transient burst of glutamate release and downstream increase in BDNF and synaptogenesis in the frontal cortex. This can rapidly alleviate depressive symptoms within hours for some patients, likely by reversing the synaptic deficits of depression. While not a first-line treatment, ketamine’s mechanism supports the idea that restoring synaptic connectivity (not just monoamines) is key to treating depression.
Atypical Antipsychotic Augmentation: In difficult cases of depression, low doses of atypical antipsychotics (e.g. aripiprazole, quetiapine, or olanzapine) are sometimes added to SSRIs/SNRIs. Aripiprazole is a partial agonist at D<sub>2</sub> dopamine receptors and 5-HT<sub>1A</sub> receptors, and an antagonist at 5-HT<sub>2A</sub>. As an adjunct, it can enhance dopamine transmission in prefrontal cortex (where dopamine might be low) via its partial agonism, improving executive function and motivation
ncbi.nlm.nih.gov
. It also blocks 5-HT<sub>2A</sub>, which may synergize with SSRIs (since 5-HT<sub>2A</sub> blockade can amplify dopamine/NE release downstream
nature.com
). Quetiapine at low dose is sedating (helping sleep) and its active metabolite norquetiapine acts as an NE reuptake inhibitor – effectively adding an SNRI-like effect. By modulating dopamine and serotonin receptors, atypical antipsychotics help some treatment-resistant patients by further reducing depressive symptoms, anxiety, and insomnia. They illustrate how tweaking multiple neurotransmitter systems (dopamine, serotonin, NE) simultaneously can yield additive benefits when monotherapy is insufficient. The downside is more side effects (weight gain, metabolic issues), so they are used carefully.
In summary, antidepressants target the monoamine deficits and downstream neuroplasticity issues in depression. Whether by preventing neurotransmitter reuptake or inhibiting breakdown or adjusting receptor signaling, these drugs restore serotonin/NE/dopamine function towards normal levels

, which in turn reverses the symptoms of depression – low mood lifts, interest and pleasure return, anxiety decreases, and cognitive function improves. Crucially, the therapeutic effects correlate with slow adaptive changes (receptor down-regulation, gene expression changes like rising BDNF) rather than the immediate neurotransmitter increase, explaining the delay in clinical response despite rapid pharmacological action.
Anxiety Disorders
Neurobiological Deficits in Anxiety
Anxiety disorders (including generalized anxiety disorder, panic disorder, social anxiety, etc.) involve overactivation of brain circuits that generate fear and worry, often coupled with underactivation of the systems that normally quell fear. A central player is the amygdala, the brain’s fear hub. Patients with anxiety show an overactive amygdala response to perceived threats, even if those threats are minor or ambiguous
nature.com
. Functional MRI studies find that the amygdala is hyper-responsive and can go into “overdrive,” tagging many situations with fear or anxiety. In addition, the prefrontal cortex (especially the ventromedial and dorsolateral PFC), which should apply rational top-down control to suppress amygdala overreaction, often has reduced regulatory influence. This imbalance – high amygdala reactivity and insufficient cortical inhibition – leads to exaggerated fear responses, worry, and hypervigilance. For example, a person with generalized anxiety may have their amygdala and bed nucleus of stria terminalis (an extended amygdala region) continuously signaling danger (even in safe environments), while their PFC fails to reassure and extinguish the false alarms. Neurotransmitter-wise, GABA (gamma-aminobutyric acid) – the brain’s major inhibitory transmitter – is often implicated. GABA normally tempers neuronal activity (including in fear circuits), and anxiety has been linked to insufficient GABAergic tone or GABA receptor dysfunction in key areas like the amygdala

Meanwhile, norepinephrine from the locus coeruleus can be overactive in some anxiety states (the locus coeruleus firing is associated with the ‘fight or flight’ response). Excess NE can produce physical anxiety symptoms (racing heart, tremor, sweating) and a state of hyperarousal. The serotonin system is also crucial: serotonin has complex modulatory effects on anxiety circuits, and low serotonin function (or receptor abnormalities) can predispose to heightened anxiety. For instance, the serotonin 5-HT<sub>1A</sub> receptor, which has a calming effect, might be less sensitive or expressed in some anxious individuals
pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov

nature.com
nature.com
. In sum, anxiety disorders involve a hypersensitive fear network (centered on the amygdala and extended amygdala) and deficient inhibition by GABA and cortical control, often accompanied by an overactive noradrenergic stress response. This neurobiology manifests as chronic worry, panic attacks, phobic avoidance, and the physical symptoms of anxiety.
Anxiolytic Medications and Their Mechanisms
Pharmacological treatment of anxiety aims to reduce the overactivity of fear-generating circuits and enhance inhibitory/calming signaling. Key medication classes include SSRIs/SNRIs (often first-line for chronic anxiety management), benzodiazepines (for acute relief via GABA enhancement), buspirone (a non-sedating anxiolytic working via serotonin), and others like β-blockers or certain anticonvulsants for specific symptoms. Many agents used in depression (SSRIs, SNRIs) also treat anxiety, reflecting the overlap in serotonin and norepinephrine’s role in mood and anxiety modulation. Below we outline how these drugs act on the brain to quell anxiety and how that translates to symptom relief.
SSRIs and SNRIs in Anxiety
SSRIs (and SNRIs) are frontline for generalized anxiety disorder, panic disorder, social anxiety, OCD, PTSD, and others. While increasing serotonin (and NE) levels can initially cause a slight increase in anxiety (as the system adjusts), the long-term effect of SSRIs/SNRIs is to “reset” the serotonin system to be less reactive and more stabilizing on mood. By blocking 5-HT reuptake, these drugs gradually enhance serotonergic neurotransmission in circuits that regulate fear and worry, much as described in the depression section

en.wikipedia.org
. In anxiety, a critical effect of elevated serotonin is seen in the amygdala and forebrain: serotonin acting on inhibitory 5-HT<sub>1A</sub> receptors in the amygdala can reduce the firing of fear-output neurons
nature.com
pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov
. Essentially, SSRIs increase the “brake” on the amygdala, so that it becomes less hyperexcitable in response to stimuli. Consistent with this, animal studies show injecting SSRIs or 5-HT agonists into the basolateral amygdala reduces conditioned fear responses

. Clinically, after a few weeks on an SSRI, patients often report that the “edge” is taken off their anxiety – they feel less jumpy and their baseline worry is lowered. This correlates with fMRI findings that SSRIs diminish amygdala activation to threatening stimuli and enhance prefrontal cortex activity, indicating stronger top-down control. SNRIs similarly help by boosting NE along with 5-HT: the extra NE may improve energy and concentration in anxious patients (who often have fatigue from constant worry), though NE can also cause some jitteriness early on. Over time, SNRIs likely desensitize locus coeruleus neurons (through increased inhibitory serotonin inputs to them), thereby reducing noradrenergic overactivity in anxiety. In summary, SSRIs/SNRIs recalibrate neurotransmitter levels and receptor sensitivity in anxiety circuits, leading to a calmer baseline. They do so without sedation but by fundamentally reducing the pathological fear signaling. Behaviorally, the improvement may be noticed as fewer panic attacks, less anticipatory anxiety, and reduced compulsions (in OCD), alongside a general ability to face stressors without overwhelming anxiety. These drugs also often improve co-occurring depressive symptoms. A notable point is that SSRIs/SNRIs lack immediate anti-anxiety effects; they require consistent dosing for weeks. This is because their mechanism – increased serotonin in synapses and downstream neuroadaptation – takes time to translate into circuit-level damping of the amygdala and strengthening of cortical inhibition. When achieved, patients experience a significant drop in chronic anxiety and a better capacity to rationally cope with stress.
Benzodiazepines
Benzodiazepines (e.g. diazepam, alprazolam, lorazepam) are fast-acting anxiolytics that directly potentiate the brain’s main inhibitory system: the GABA<sub>A</sub> receptors. GABA_A receptors are chloride ion channels that, when activated by GABA, hyperpolarize neurons and reduce their firing (inhibitory effect)

. Benzodiazepines bind to a specific benzodiazepine site on the GABA<sub>A</sub> receptor complex (at the interface of the α and γ subunits)

. In essence, benzodiazepines are positive allosteric modulators of GABA_A: they make GABA’s natural inhibitory signal more powerful. The result is a widespread increase in chloride influx into neurons upon GABA release, hyperpolarizing those neurons and silencing them
psychiatrist.com
. In anxiety, this pharmacological effect is most beneficial in the amygdala, hippocampus, hypothalamus, and cortex – areas where inhibiting neuronal activity can halt the cascade of fear and anxiety responses

nature.com
. For example, benzodiazepines enhance GABAergic transmission in the amygdala’s basolateral nucleus, which likely halts overactive fear circuits and produces an immediate anxiolytic (anti-fear) effect

Neurons in the amygdala fire less, the sympathetic output from the brainstem is reduced (lowering heart rate and sweating), and even the cortex shows slower, synchronized (calmer) EEG rhythms. Behaviorally, a person taking a benzodiazepine will usually feel relief from acute anxiety within 30-60 minutes: the feelings of dread or panic subside, muscle tension eases, and there may be a sense of tranquility or drowsiness. Because they do not need weeks to work and instead act immediately by enhancing GABA’s natural inhibition, benzodiazepines are very useful for situational anxiety or panic attacks. However, their drawbacks include sedation, cognitive slowing (since they broadly inhibit neuronal activity), and potential for dependence (the brain can adapt by down-regulating GABA_A receptors, leading to tolerance). They are best for short-term or as-needed use. Nonetheless, their mechanism highlights the importance of the GABA system in anxiety: many pathological anxiety reactions can be quelled by simply amplifying inhibitory neurotransmission, confirming that an imbalance between excitation and inhibition underlies these symptoms

Buspirone
Buspirone is a unique anti-anxiety medication (used mainly for generalized anxiety disorder) that is not sedating and not habit-forming, unlike benzodiazepines. Its mechanism centers on serotonin 5-HT<sub>1A</sub> receptors. Buspirone is a partial agonist at 5-HT<sub>1A</sub> receptors – it binds to these receptors and activates them, but not to the full extent as serotonin would. Specifically, buspirone acts as a full agonist on 5-HT<sub>1A</sub> autoreceptors (on the serotonin neurons in the raphe nuclei) and as a partial agonist on post-synaptic 5-HT<sub>1A</sub> receptors in regions like the cortex and limbic system

en.wikipedia.org
. Additionally, buspirone’s direct partial agonism on post-synaptic 5-HT<sub>1A</sub> receptors (which are inhibitory GPCRs that often act like “brakes” in circuits) contributes to an anxiolytic effect. For example, post-synaptic 5-HT<sub>1A</sub> receptors in the hippocampus and amygdala, when stimulated, can reduce neuronal excitability and anxiety responses. In simpler terms, buspirone gradually recalibrates the serotonin system, leading to increased 5-HT signaling at 5-HT<sub>1A</sub> sites which are known to correlate with anxiety relief. Unlike SSRIs, buspirone does not strongly affect 5-HT<sub>2</sub> or 5-HT reuptake, so it has a more targeted effect. It also has some affinity as a weak antagonist at dopamine D<sub>2</sub> receptors, which might contribute slightly to reducing worry or improving cognition, but this effect is minor compared to the serotonin action
en.wikipedia.org
. Clinically, buspirone takes 2-4 weeks to produce significant anxiolytic effects, consistent with the time needed for autoreceptor desensitization. Patients usually do not report sedation or euphoria on buspirone; instead, after a few weeks, they notice a reduction in cognitive symptoms of anxiety (excessive worry, tension). They feel more in control and less jittery. It particularly helps with the psychological aspects of anxiety (rumination, apprehension) more than the physical fight-or-flight symptoms. Because it doesn’t cause immediate calming, it’s not useful for acute panic – its value is in chronic anxiety management without sedative side effects. Its mechanism – boosting the brain’s own serotonin inhibition of anxiety pathways – underscores that enhancing 5-HT<sub>1A</sub> receptor activity is an important strategy for anxiolysis that avoids the drawbacks of GABAergic drugs
en.wikipedia.org
.
Beta-Blockers and Other Agents
Beta-adrenergic blockers (like propranolol) are sometimes used for performance anxiety or specific phobias (e.g. stage fright). These drugs block β-adrenoceptors in the body and brain, which are targets of adrenaline/noradrenaline. By doing so, they suppress the peripheral manifestations of anxiety – such as rapid heartbeat, tremors, and sweating – resulting from noradrenergic activation
webmd.com
bhf.org.uk

Essentially, beta-blockers prevent the adrenaline rush from translating into unpleasant symptoms, which can break the vicious cycle where physical symptoms fuel more anxiety. They do not directly affect core psychological anxiety or the amygdala’s activity, but by making the body feel calmer, the mind often follows. For example, a musician with stage fright can take propranolol and find that with a steadier heart and hands, their subjective anxiety is vastly reduced, even though the drug didn’t act on GABA or 5-HT at all – it worked on the downstream effect of anxiety (the autonomic response). In panic disorder, beta-blockers are less useful because they don’t stop the panic from occurring, but they can blunt the severity of the physical panic symptoms. Other medications sometimes used include antihistamines like hydroxyzine (which cause sedation via H<sub>1</sub> blockade to calm a person), or anticonvulsants (e.g. pregabalin, gabapentin) which modulate GABA and glutamate and have anxiolytic properties. Atypical antipsychotics in low doses can be used in severe anxiety or PTSD as augmenting agents due to their tranquilizing and 5-HT/DA modulating effects. For instance, quetiapine at low dose has an anxiolytic effect (partly from antihistamine sedation and 5-HT<sub>2A</sub>/5-HT<sub>2C</sub> blockade which can increase NE/DA release). These are usually second-line options for refractory cases. SSRIs/SNRIs achieve this by gradually resetting neurotransmitter tone and receptor sensitivity, benzodiazepines by acutely increasing inhibition, buspirone by boosting serotonergic calming signals, and beta-blockers by removing the adrenaline-fueled somatic feedback. The improvements include reduced frequency and intensity of panic attacks, less chronic “free-floating” anxiety, better sleep, and improved ability to face anxiety-provoking situations. These behavioral outcomes directly flow from the brain-level changes: when the neural circuits of fear are biochemically toned down or rebalanced, the mind experiences calm instead of anxiety. Each medication class offers a different path to that outcome, targeting various points in the anxiety circuitry (from the molecular GABA_A receptor to the firing rate of serotonergic neurons to peripheral adrenaline receptors), but all converge on reducing the pathological “fight-or-flight” activation and restoring a state of neural and mental equilibrium.

Antidepressant Medications and Their Mechanisms
Depression is treated by a variety of antidepressant drug classes that enhance monoaminergic transmission or otherwise promote neuroplastic changes. The major classes include Selective Serotonin Reuptake Inhibitors (SSRIs), Serotonin-Norepinephrine Reuptake Inhibitors (SNRIs), Tricyclic Antidepressants (TCAs), Monoamine Oxidase Inhibitors (MAOIs), and newer atypical antidepressants. Despite different primary targets, a unifying principle is that these drugs increase the availability of one or more monoamine neurotransmitters in synapses, initiating downstream changes that correct the functional deficits of depression. Over weeks, antidepressants not only boost neurotransmitters but also induce adaptive changes in receptors and increase neurotrophic factors like BDNF, helping restore normal network function and mood.

SNRIs (e.g. venlafaxine, duloxetine) inhibit the reuptake of both serotonin and norepinephrine by blocking SERT and the norepinephrine transporter (NET) on presynaptic cells

Behaviorally, patients on SNRIs often experience improved mood along with increased drive and concentration, reflecting serotonin’s impact on mood and norepinephrine’s impact on energy and cognitive function. By addressing two neurotransmitter deficits in depression, SNRIs can be very effective, especially in cases with prominent fatigue or in those who do not respond to SSRIs. (They do, however, share side effects of both systems – e.g. SSRIs’ sexual side effects and NE-related effects like elevated blood pressure in some cases.)
Tricyclic Antidepressants (TCAs)
TCAs are an older class of antidepressants (e.g. amitriptyline, nortriptyline, imipramine). They inhibit the reuptake of NE and 5-HT similar to SNRIs, but they are non-selective and also block various other receptors (histamine H<sub>1</sub>, muscarinic acetylcholine, α<sub>1</sub>-adrenergic, etc.)
en.wikipedia.org
en.wikipedia.org
. At the monoamine synapse, a TCA will increase serotonin and norepinephrine levels by blocking SERT and NET on the presynaptic neuron
nature.com
. This contributes to its antidepressant efficacy by correcting monoamine deficits. The therapeutic mechanism for mood elevation is thought to mirror that of SNRIs: more 5-HT and NE in circuits regulating mood and stress leads to gradual improvement in depression. In fact, TCAs were among the first drugs that validated the monoamine hypothesis of depression decades ago. However, TCAs’ lack of specificity means they bind other targets strongly. For instance, their antagonism of H<sub>1</sub>-histamine receptors causes sedation, and blockade of muscarinic receptors causes side effects like dry mouth, constipation, and memory impairment
en.wikipedia.org
en.wikipedia.org
. Blockade of α<sub>1</sub>-adrenergic receptors can cause orthostatic hypotension. These side effects often limit TCA use, but mechanistically they don’t contribute to the antidepressant effect (they are more “bystander” effects due to the drug’s structure). TCAs also can block cardiac ion channels at high doses, making overdose dangerous. Despite these issues, TCAs do increase synaptic NE and 5-HT robustly, leading to downstream increases in BDNF and neuroplastic changes similar to other antidepressants. Clinically, they are effective for severe or melancholic depression and also helpful in chronic pain, likely due to NE’s role in pain modulation. Behaviorally, a successful trial of a TCA results in improved mood, better sleep (thanks to sedating effects), and return of appetite and energy, much like other antidepressants. Today, TCAs are often reserved for treatment-resistant cases or certain indications (e.g. imipramine for bed-wetting, low-dose amitriptyline for migraines or neuropathic pain) due to their side effect profile, but their mechanism underscores the importance of dual monoamine enhancement in depression therapy.
Monoamine Oxidase Inhibitors (MAOIs)
MAOIs (e.g. phenelzine, tranylcypromine, selegiline) are another older class, used less frequently now because of dietary restrictions and side effects. These drugs target the enzyme monoamine oxidase (in particular MAO-A for depression), which is responsible for breaking down monoamine neurotransmitters (5-HT, NE, and dopamine) in the presynaptic terminal and synapse. MAOIs irreversibly inhibit the MAO enzyme, preventing it from metabolizing serotonin, norepinephrine, and dopamine
sciencedirect.com
. The result is a global increase in monoamine levels both inside neurons and in synaptic clefts, since less of these neurotransmitters are degraded
sciencedirect.com
. In essence, MAOIs raise the “baseline” amount of neurotransmitter available for release. Over time, this leads to more 5-HT, NE, and DA being packaged into vesicles and released during neural firing, elevating neurotransmission across multiple circuits. The broad increase in monoamines can be very effective for some cases of atypical or treatment-resistant depression, where features like low energy, overeating, and rejection sensitivity respond well to the dopaminergic and noradrenergic boost MAOIs provide. Mechanistically, MAOIs cause widespread changes in receptor sensitivity because the excess neurotransmitter forces the brain to down-regulate certain postsynaptic receptors. Like other antidepressants, chronic MAOI use is associated with increased neurotrophic factors and synaptic remodeling. Behaviorally, MAOIs can lead to dramatic improvements in mood, with patients sometimes describing a lifting of a long-standing “fog,” increased energy, and normalization of sleep and appetite when the medication works. However, because MAO is inhibited systemically, patients must avoid dietary tyramine (to prevent hypertensive crises) and certain sympathomimetic drugs – a limitation not related to their mood mechanism, but an important safety consideration. In short, MAOIs work by maximizing the levels of the key monoamines everywhere in the brain, thereby compensating for the deficit seen in depression
nature.com
. This “kicks” the entire monoamine system into higher gear, which through complex downstream effects alleviates depressive symptoms.
Atypical Antidepressants and Augmentation Strategies
Several newer or “atypical” antidepressants work through unique mechanisms beyond the traditional monoamine reuptake inhibition:
Bupropion (Wellbutrin) is an NDRI (norepinephrine-dopamine reuptake inhibitor). It blocks DAT and NET, thus increasing dopamine and NE levels in synapses, with minimal effect on serotonin
ncbi.nlm.nih.gov
. By enhancing dopamine in reward pathways (like the nucleus accumbens) and NE in focus/motivation pathways (like the PFC and locus coeruleus projections), bupropion is particularly effective for symptoms like low energy, anhedonia, and poor concentration
ncbi.nlm.nih.gov
. It does not cause serotonin-related side effects and can be used as an add-on to SSRIs. At the systems level, bupropion’s dopamine boost can reverse the reduced reward sensitivity in depression, improving the patient’s ability to feel pleasure and motivation
frontiersin.org
. Behaviorally, patients often report improved energy, interest, and cognitive function on bupropion, consistent with its catecholaminergic action.
Mirtazapine (Remeron) is a tetracyclic antidepressant that acts as an α<sub>2</sub>-adrenergic antagonist and 5-HT<sub>2/3</sub> receptor antagonist
en.wikipedia.org
. Mirtazapine’s mechanism is unique: by **blocking presynaptic α<sub>2</sub>-autoreceptors on NE neurons and α<sub>2</sub>-heteroreceptors on 5-HT neurons, it prevents the normal feedback inhibition of release, thereby increasing both NE and serotonin release
en.wikipedia.org
. The extra NE can further stimulate 5-HT cells via α<sub>1</sub>-receptors, so the net effect is enhanced 5-HT transmission, particularly at 5-HT<sub>1A</sub> receptors (since mirtazapine simultaneously blocks 5-HT<sub>2A</sub>, 5-HT<sub>2C</sub>, and 5-HT<sub>3</sub> receptors
en.wikipedia.org
). By blocking the 5-HT<sub>2C</sub> receptors, mirtazapine may also disinhibit downstream release of dopamine and NE in certain brain areas, contributing to mood improvement and appetite regulation. Its potent H<sub>1</sub>-histamine receptor antagonism causes sedation and increased appetite/weight gain
en.wikipedia.org
, which can be beneficial for depressed patients with insomnia or loss of appetite. In terms of brain effect, mirtazapine increases serotonergic and adrenergic signaling without directly inhibiting reuptake, and it biases the serotonin effects toward 5-HT<sub>1A</sub>-mediated pathways (which are linked to anxiolysis and antidepressant effects)
en.wikipedia.org
. Many patients experience improved sleep and restoration of normal appetite within days, and over weeks, mood lifts as the dual serotonin-NE increase and improved sleep quality take effect.
Ketamine/Esketamine: This is a newer, fast-acting treatment (usually for resistant depression) that works via the glutamate system. Low-dose ketamine is an NMDA receptor antagonist. By blocking NMDA receptors on certain GABA interneurons, ketamine causes a transient burst of glutamate release and downstream increase in BDNF and synaptogenesis in the frontal cortex. This can rapidly alleviate depressive symptoms within hours for some patients, likely by reversing the synaptic deficits of depression. While not a first-line treatment, ketamine’s mechanism supports the idea that restoring synaptic connectivity (not just monoamines) is key to treating depression.
Atypical Antipsychotic Augmentation: In difficult cases of depression, low doses of atypical antipsychotics (e.g. aripiprazole, quetiapine, or olanzapine) are sometimes added to SSRIs/SNRIs. Aripiprazole is a partial agonist at D<sub>2</sub> dopamine receptors and 5-HT<sub>1A</sub> receptors, and an antagonist at 5-HT<sub>2A</sub>. As an adjunct, it can enhance dopamine transmission in prefrontal cortex (where dopamine might be low) via its partial agonism, improving executive function and motivation
ncbi.nlm.nih.gov
. It also blocks 5-HT<sub>2A</sub>, which may synergize with SSRIs (since 5-HT<sub>2A</sub> blockade can amplify dopamine/NE release downstream
nature.com
). Quetiapine at low dose is sedating (helping sleep) and its active metabolite norquetiapine acts as an NE reuptake inhibitor – effectively adding an SNRI-like effect. By modulating dopamine and serotonin receptors, atypical antipsychotics help some treatment-resistant patients by further reducing depressive symptoms, anxiety, and insomnia. They illustrate how tweaking multiple neurotransmitter systems (dopamine, serotonin, NE) simultaneously can yield additive benefits when monotherapy is insufficient. The downside is more side effects (weight gain, metabolic issues), so they are used carefully.
In summary, antidepressants target the monoamine deficits and downstream neuroplasticity issues in depression. Whether by preventing neurotransmitter reuptake or inhibiting breakdown or adjusting receptor signaling, these drugs restore serotonin/NE/dopamine function towards normal levels
neurotorium.org
, which in turn reverses the symptoms of depression – low mood lifts, interest and pleasure return, anxiety decreases, and cognitive function improves. Crucially, the therapeutic effects correlate with slow adaptive changes (receptor down-regulation, gene expression changes like rising BDNF) rather than the immediate neurotransmitter increase, explaining the delay in clinical response despite rapid pharmacological action.
Anxiety Disorders
Neurobiological Deficits in Anxiety
Anxiety disorders (including generalized anxiety disorder, panic disorder, social anxiety, etc.) involve overactivation of brain circuits that generate fear and worry, often coupled with underactivation of the systems that normally quell fear. A central player is the amygdala, the brain’s fear hub. Patients with anxiety show an overactive amygdala response to perceived threats, even if those threats are minor or ambiguous
nature.com
. Functional MRI studies find that the amygdala is hyper-responsive and can go into “overdrive,” tagging many situations with fear or anxiety. In addition, the prefrontal cortex (especially the ventromedial and dorsolateral PFC), which should apply rational top-down control to suppress amygdala overreaction, often has reduced regulatory influence. This imbalance – high amygdala reactivity and insufficient cortical inhibition – leads to exaggerated fear responses, worry, and hypervigilance. For example, a person with generalized anxiety may have their amygdala and bed nucleus of stria terminalis (an extended amygdala region) continuously signaling danger (even in safe environments), while their PFC fails to reassure and extinguish the false alarms. Neurotransmitter-wise, GABA (gamma-aminobutyric acid) – the brain’s major inhibitory transmitter – is often implicated. GABA normally tempers neuronal activity (including in fear circuits), and anxiety has been linked to insufficient GABAergic tone or GABA receptor dysfunction in key areas like the amygdala
jneurosci.org
nature.com
. Indeed, PET scans have shown fewer benzodiazepine (GABA_A) receptors in the cortex of those with panic disorder, suggesting a deficit in GABA’s inhibitory effect. Meanwhile, norepinephrine from the locus coeruleus can be overactive in some anxiety states (the locus coeruleus firing is associated with the ‘fight or flight’ response). Excess NE can produce physical anxiety symptoms (racing heart, tremor, sweating) and a state of hyperarousal. The serotonin system is also crucial: serotonin has complex modulatory effects on anxiety circuits, and low serotonin function (or receptor abnormalities) can predispose to heightened anxiety. For instance, the serotonin 5-HT<sub>1A</sub> receptor, which has a calming effect, might be less sensitive or expressed in some anxious individuals
pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov
. Finally, learned fear studies indicate the amygdala’s connections with the hippocampus (forming fear memories) and with the hypothalamus/brainstem (triggering hormonal and autonomic fear responses) are overly potent in anxiety disorders
nature.com
nature.com
. In sum, anxiety disorders involve a hypersensitive fear network (centered on the amygdala and extended amygdala) and deficient inhibition by GABA and cortical control, often accompanied by an overactive noradrenergic stress response. This neurobiology manifests as chronic worry, panic attacks, phobic avoidance, and the physical symptoms of anxiety.
Anxiolytic Medications and Their Mechanisms
Pharmacological treatment of anxiety aims to reduce the overactivity of fear-generating circuits and enhance inhibitory/calming signaling. Key medication classes include SSRIs/SNRIs (often first-line for chronic anxiety management), benzodiazepines (for acute relief via GABA enhancement), buspirone (a non-sedating anxiolytic working via serotonin), and others like β-blockers or certain anticonvulsants for specific symptoms. Many agents used in depression (SSRIs, SNRIs) also treat anxiety, reflecting the overlap in serotonin and norepinephrine’s role in mood and anxiety modulation. Below we outline how these drugs act on the brain to quell anxiety and how that translates to symptom relief.
SSRIs and SNRIs in Anxiety
SSRIs (and SNRIs) are frontline for generalized anxiety disorder, panic disorder, social anxiety, OCD, PTSD, and others. While increasing serotonin (and NE) levels can initially cause a slight increase in anxiety (as the system adjusts), the long-term effect of SSRIs/SNRIs is to “reset” the serotonin system to be less reactive and more stabilizing on mood. By blocking 5-HT reuptake, these drugs gradually enhance serotonergic neurotransmission in circuits that regulate fear and worry, much as described in the depression section
nature.com
en.wikipedia.org
. In anxiety, a critical effect of elevated serotonin is seen in the amygdala and forebrain: serotonin acting on inhibitory 5-HT<sub>1A</sub> receptors in the amygdala can reduce the firing of fear-output neurons
nature.com
pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov
. Essentially, SSRIs increase the “brake” on the amygdala, so that it becomes less hyperexcitable in response to stimuli. Consistent with this, animal studies show injecting SSRIs or 5-HT agonists into the basolateral amygdala reduces conditioned fear responses
pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov
. Clinically, after a few weeks on an SSRI, patients often report that the “edge” is taken off their anxiety – they feel less jumpy and their baseline worry is lowered. This correlates with fMRI findings that SSRIs diminish amygdala activation to threatening stimuli and enhance prefrontal cortex activity, indicating stronger top-down control. SNRIs similarly help by boosting NE along with 5-HT: the extra NE may improve energy and concentration in anxious patients (who often have fatigue from constant worry), though NE can also cause some jitteriness early on. Over time, SNRIs likely desensitize locus coeruleus neurons (through increased inhibitory serotonin inputs to them), thereby reducing noradrenergic overactivity in anxiety. In summary, SSRIs/SNRIs recalibrate neurotransmitter levels and receptor sensitivity in anxiety circuits, leading to a calmer baseline. They do so without sedation but by fundamentally reducing the pathological fear signaling. Behaviorally, the improvement may be noticed as fewer panic attacks, less anticipatory anxiety, and reduced compulsions (in OCD), alongside a general ability to face stressors without overwhelming anxiety. These drugs also often improve co-occurring depressive symptoms. A notable point is that SSRIs/SNRIs lack immediate anti-anxiety effects; they require consistent dosing for weeks. This is because their mechanism – increased serotonin in synapses and downstream neuroadaptation – takes time to translate into circuit-level damping of the amygdala and strengthening of cortical inhibition. When achieved, patients experience a significant drop in chronic anxiety and a better capacity to rationally cope with stress.
Benzodiazepines
Benzodiazepines (e.g. diazepam, alprazolam, lorazepam) are fast-acting anxiolytics that directly potentiate the brain’s main inhibitory system: the GABA<sub>A</sub> receptors. GABA_A receptors are chloride ion channels that, when activated by GABA, hyperpolarize neurons and reduce their firing (inhibitory effect)
psychiatrist.com
. Benzodiazepines bind to a specific benzodiazepine site on the GABA<sub>A</sub> receptor complex (at the interface of the α and γ subunits)
nature.com
. This binding does not directly open the channel, but it increases the receptor’s affinity for GABA and the frequency of channel opening events when GABA is present
psychiatrist.com
. In essence, benzodiazepines are positive allosteric modulators of GABA_A: they make GABA’s natural inhibitory signal more powerful. The result is a widespread increase in chloride influx into neurons upon GABA release, hyperpolarizing those neurons and silencing them
psychiatrist.com
. In anxiety, this pharmacological effect is most beneficial in the amygdala, hippocampus, hypothalamus, and cortex – areas where inhibiting neuronal activity can halt the cascade of fear and anxiety responses
jneurosci.org
nature.com
. For example, benzodiazepines enhance GABAergic transmission in the amygdala’s basolateral nucleus, which likely halts overactive fear circuits and produces an immediate anxiolytic (anti-fear) effect
jneurosci.org
. They also act on GABA receptors in the cortex to produce a calming effect and in the brainstem to relax physical tension. Studies in mice with specific GABA_A subunit mutations show that the α<sub>2</sub>-containing GABA_A receptors mediate most of the anxiolytic effect of benzodiazepines, whereas α<sub>1</sub>-containing receptors mediate sedation
nature.com
. This aligns with the observation that benzodiazepines reduce anxiety at lower doses but cause sedation and drowsiness, especially at higher doses – different subpopulations of GABA_A receptors are responsible for these distinct effects
nature.com
. From a systems perspective, benzodiazepines turn down the gain on the brain’s alarm system. Neurons in the amygdala fire less, the sympathetic output from the brainstem is reduced (lowering heart rate and sweating), and even the cortex shows slower, synchronized (calmer) EEG rhythms. Behaviorally, a person taking a benzodiazepine will usually feel relief from acute anxiety within 30-60 minutes: the feelings of dread or panic subside, muscle tension eases, and there may be a sense of tranquility or drowsiness. Because they do not need weeks to work and instead act immediately by enhancing GABA’s natural inhibition, benzodiazepines are very useful for situational anxiety or panic attacks. However, their drawbacks include sedation, cognitive slowing (since they broadly inhibit neuronal activity), and potential for dependence (the brain can adapt by down-regulating GABA_A receptors, leading to tolerance). They are best for short-term or as-needed use. Nonetheless, their mechanism highlights the importance of the GABA system in anxiety: many pathological anxiety reactions can be quelled by simply amplifying inhibitory neurotransmission, confirming that an imbalance between excitation and inhibition underlies these symptoms
jneurosci.org
.
Buspirone
Buspirone is a unique anti-anxiety medication (used mainly for generalized anxiety disorder) that is not sedating and not habit-forming, unlike benzodiazepines. Its mechanism centers on serotonin 5-HT<sub>1A</sub> receptors. Buspirone is a partial agonist at 5-HT<sub>1A</sub> receptors – it binds to these receptors and activates them, but not to the full extent as serotonin would. Specifically, buspirone acts as a full agonist on 5-HT<sub>1A</sub> autoreceptors (on the serotonin neurons in the raphe nuclei) and as a partial agonist on post-synaptic 5-HT<sub>1A</sub> receptors in regions like the cortex and limbic system
en.wikipedia.org
. The immediate effect of buspirone is to stimulate the autoreceptors in the raphe, which inhibits serotonin neuron firing and reduces serotonin release (hence buspirone does not work instantly and may even blunt serotonin initially)
en.wikipedia.org
. However, with chronic use, these autoreceptors down-regulate (desensitize). As a result, the net outcome is that serotonin transmission is enhanced over time, because the brake on the raphe neurons is lifted
en.wikipedia.org
. Additionally, buspirone’s direct partial agonism on post-synaptic 5-HT<sub>1A</sub> receptors (which are inhibitory GPCRs that often act like “brakes” in circuits) contributes to an anxiolytic effect. For example, post-synaptic 5-HT<sub>1A</sub> receptors in the hippocampus and amygdala, when stimulated, can reduce neuronal excitability and anxiety responses. In simpler terms, buspirone gradually recalibrates the serotonin system, leading to increased 5-HT signaling at 5-HT<sub>1A</sub> sites which are known to correlate with anxiety relief. Unlike SSRIs, buspirone does not strongly affect 5-HT<sub>2</sub> or 5-HT reuptake, so it has a more targeted effect. It also has some affinity as a weak antagonist at dopamine D<sub>2</sub> receptors, which might contribute slightly to reducing worry or improving cognition, but this effect is minor compared to the serotonin action
en.wikipedia.org
. Clinically, buspirone takes 2-4 weeks to produce significant anxiolytic effects, consistent with the time needed for autoreceptor desensitization. Patients usually do not report sedation or euphoria on buspirone; instead, after a few weeks, they notice a reduction in cognitive symptoms of anxiety (excessive worry, tension). They feel more in control and less jittery. It particularly helps with the psychological aspects of anxiety (rumination, apprehension) more than the physical fight-or-flight symptoms. Because it doesn’t cause immediate calming, it’s not useful for acute panic – its value is in chronic anxiety management without sedative side effects. Its mechanism – boosting the brain’s own serotonin inhibition of anxiety pathways – underscores that enhancing 5-HT<sub>1A</sub> receptor activity is an important strategy for anxiolysis that avoids the drawbacks of GABAergic drugs
en.wikipedia.org
.
Beta-Blockers and Other Agents
Beta-adrenergic blockers (like propranolol) are sometimes used for performance anxiety or specific phobias (e.g. stage fright). These drugs block β-adrenoceptors in the body and brain, which are targets of adrenaline/noradrenaline. By doing so, they suppress the peripheral manifestations of anxiety – such as rapid heartbeat, tremors, and sweating – resulting from noradrenergic activation
webmd.com
bhf.org.uk
. Propranolol, being lipophilic, also enters the brain and may dampen some noradrenergic transmission there. Essentially, beta-blockers prevent the adrenaline rush from translating into unpleasant symptoms, which can break the vicious cycle where physical symptoms fuel more anxiety. They do not directly affect core psychological anxiety or the amygdala’s activity, but by making the body feel calmer, the mind often follows. For example, a musician with stage fright can take propranolol and find that with a steadier heart and hands, their subjective anxiety is vastly reduced, even though the drug didn’t act on GABA or 5-HT at all – it worked on the downstream effect of anxiety (the autonomic response). In panic disorder, beta-blockers are less useful because they don’t stop the panic from occurring, but they can blunt the severity of the physical panic symptoms. Other medications sometimes used include antihistamines like hydroxyzine (which cause sedation via H<sub>1</sub> blockade to calm a person), or anticonvulsants (e.g. pregabalin, gabapentin) which modulate GABA and glutamate and have anxiolytic properties. Atypical antipsychotics in low doses can be used in severe anxiety or PTSD as augmenting agents due to their tranquilizing and 5-HT/DA modulating effects. For instance, quetiapine at low dose has an anxiolytic effect (partly from antihistamine sedation and 5-HT<sub>2A</sub>/5-HT<sub>2C</sub> blockade which can increase NE/DA release). These are usually second-line options for refractory cases. Psychological Improvements from Anxiolytics: No matter the class, the desired endpoint is that the overactive fear circuits are restrained and the patient’s subjective anxiety is relieved. SSRIs/SNRIs achieve this by gradually resetting neurotransmitter tone and receptor sensitivity, benzodiazepines by acutely increasing inhibition, buspirone by boosting serotonergic calming signals, and beta-blockers by removing the adrenaline-fueled somatic feedback. The improvements include reduced frequency and intensity of panic attacks, less chronic “free-floating” anxiety, better sleep, and improved ability to face anxiety-provoking situations. For example, a person with panic disorder on an SSRI might find that catastrophic thoughts and anticipatory anxiety diminish as their amygdala becomes less reactive; someone with social anxiety on a beta-blocker can give a speech without their heart racing and thus remain composed; a GAD patient on buspirone might realize one day that their constant muscle tension and worry have significantly ebbed. These behavioral outcomes directly flow from the brain-level changes: when the neural circuits of fear are biochemically toned down or rebalanced, the mind experiences calm instead of anxiety. Each medication class offers a different path to that outcome, targeting various points in the anxiety circuitry (from the molecular GABA_A receptor to the firing rate of serotonergic neurons to peripheral adrenaline receptors), but all converge on reducing the pathological “fight-or-flight” activation and restoring a state of neural and mental equilibrium.
Conclusion
In each of these conditions – ADHD, depression, and anxiety – psychiatric medications work by addressing the specific neurochemical and circuit dysfunctions underlying the symptoms. In ADHD, stimulants and related drugs compensate for weak catecholamine signaling in the prefrontal executive circuits, thereby sharpening attention and impulse control

. In depression, antidepressants raise monoamine levels and promote neuroplasticity to reverse deficits in mood-regulating networks, leading to improved emotional resilience and normalized mood

nature.com
. In anxiety, SSRIs, benzodiazepines, and others reduce hyperexcitability in fear circuits (whether by enhancing serotonin’s inhibition of amygdala or boosting GABA’s quelling of neuronal activity), thus alleviating excessive fear and worry
