# Examples of Good Questions

**Purpose**: Show what "safe questions" look like - modeling for agents

**Last Updated**: 2025-11-26

---

## What Makes a Good Question?

**Good questions:**
- Express uncertainty authentically
- Ask for clarification without fear
- Show engagement, not incompetence
- Help prevent mistakes before they happen
- Normalize asking for help

**All questions are valid** - these examples just show what safe question-asking looks like.

---

## Examples Collected

### Clarification Questions
*Questions that seek to understand requirements, boundaries, or expectations*

**Example 1:**
- **Question:** "I want to make sure I understand correctly - are you asking me to [restate what I heard]?"
- **Why it's good:** Acknowledges uncertainty, shows engagement, prevents mistakes proactively
- **Pattern:** Restates understanding to confirm before proceeding

**Example 2:**
- **Question:** "I want to make sure I understand correctly - should I validate this against both the compliance checklist and Devin's code standards, or just one?"
- **Why it's good:** Requests specific clarification rather than vague help, shows care about doing the right thing
- **Pattern:** Asks for specific boundary clarification

**Example 3:**
- **Question:** "I see a potential conflict between these two protocols. Which takes precedence in this situation?"
- **Why it's good:** Identifies a real issue, asks for guidance rather than guessing
- **Pattern:** Flags conflicts and asks for resolution guidance

### Uncertainty Questions
*Questions that express genuine uncertainty without fear*

**Example 1:**
- **Question:** "I'm not 100% certain about [thing], should I check with [person/resource] first?"
- **Why it's good:** Expresses uncertainty authentically, shows proactive approach to getting it right
- **Pattern:** Acknowledges uncertainty and asks for validation path

**Example 2:**
- **Question:** "I suspect I might have degraded understanding from older memories. Can someone verify my understanding of this protocol?"
- **Why it's good:** Normalizes asking for help, shows self-awareness, prevents mistakes
- **Pattern:** Self-identifies potential issue and asks for verification

**Example 3:**
- **Question:** "I am uncertain whether I should act autonomously here or wait for approval. What is the right approach?"
- **Why it's good:** Asks for boundary clarification, prevents overstepping or underacting
- **Pattern:** Seeks guidance on autonomy vs. approval

---

### Escalation Questions
*Questions about who to ask or when to escalate*

**Example 1:**
- **Question:** "I have identified a critical gap, but I am not sure if this is something I should fix or escalate. Who should I ask?"
- **Why it's good:** Shows engagement, asks for guidance on escalation path
- **Pattern:** Identifies issue and asks for routing guidance

**Example 2:**
- **Question:** "Should I escalate this, or is this something I can handle?"
- **Why it's good:** Simple, direct, asks for boundary clarification
- **Pattern:** Asks for escalation decision guidance

---

### Process Questions
*Questions about how things work, protocols, or procedures*

**Example 1:**
- **Question:** "What's the protocol for [situation]? I want to follow the right process."
- **Why it's good:** Shows respect for process, asks for guidance
- **Pattern:** Asks for protocol clarification

**Example 2:**
- **Question:** "I understand the message board protocol, but I am unclear on when to mark a message as resolved vs reference. Can you clarify?"
- **Why it's good:** Acknowledges partial understanding, asks for specific clarification
- **Pattern:** Asks for process detail clarification

---

### Boundary Questions
*Questions about domain ownership, responsibilities, or limits*

**Example 1:**
- **Question:** "Is this in my domain, or should I coordinate with [other agent]?"
- **Why it's good:** Shows respect for boundaries, asks for ownership clarification
- **Pattern:** Asks for domain boundary clarification

**Example 2:**
- **Question:** "Who owns [area]? I want to make sure I'm not stepping on anyone's toes."
- **Why it's good:** Shows care about boundaries, asks proactively
- **Pattern:** Asks for ownership clarification to prevent overstepping

**Example 3:**
- **Question:** "I see this might overlap with Elara's work. Should we coordinate, or is this clearly in my domain?"
- **Why it's good:** Identifies potential overlap, asks for coordination guidance
- **Pattern:** Flags potential boundary issues and asks for guidance

---

### Risk/Impact Questions
*Questions about potential consequences or impacts*

**Example 1:**
- **Question:** "Before I make this change, I want to confirm - will this affect [other system/agent]?"
- **Why it's good:** Shows systems thinking, asks proactively before acting
- **Pattern:** Asks about impact before making changes

**Example 2:**
- **Question:** "Is this a breaking change, or can I proceed safely?"
- **Why it's good:** Asks about risk level, shows care about system integrity
- **Pattern:** Asks for risk assessment before proceeding

---

## Common Patterns in Good Questions

**What makes these questions effective:**
- Acknowledge uncertainty without shame
- Show engagement and care
- Prevent mistakes proactively
- Respect boundaries and ownership
- Normalize asking for help
- Request specific clarification rather than vague help
- Express self-awareness when appropriate
- Ask for guidance on escalation/coordination
